# Instagram Creative Direction — هفار

> Instagram is Hvar's visual portfolio. This is where the brand aesthetic is most concentrated — where the visual direction from `brand/01_visual-direction.md` is most visibly expressed in public. Every decision here must be designed with the grid, the visual language, and the brand's premium positioning in mind.

---

## Platform Role

Instagram's specific job for Hvar is different from Facebook's community role:

- **Visual brand portfolio:** The grid is the first thing a new follower sees. It reads as a designed object. First impressions of the brand aesthetic happen here.
- **Product discovery:** Women who are visually exploring kitchen options — not yet in purchase mode — encounter Hvar through the feed, explore the profile, and either become a follower (and eventually a buyer) or not. The grid has to earn the follow.
- **Aspirational lifestyle:** Instagram is the platform where Hvar can most fully express the editorial warmth of "المطبخ الدافئ" — beautiful food photography, warm kitchen environments, the poetry of the kitchen as a space.
- **Chef culture showcase:** Chef Sara's presence on Hvar's Instagram creates credibility at a glance. The chef is visual proof of the brand's authenticity claims.
- **Younger edge of the core audience:** The 25–35 segment of Hvar's audience is heavier on Instagram than Facebook. Content here should feel contemporary without alienating the 35–45 segment.

---

## Feed Grid Strategy

The grid is read two ways: individual posts (as content) and as a composition (the brand at a glance). Design for both.

**The principle: visual rhythm, not formula**

Rigid formulas ("every 3rd post is a product post, every 4th is a recipe") produce unnatural, mechanical feeds that audiences read as inauthentic. The principle is: consistent visual atmosphere + consistent content rhythm, allowing for natural variation within that.

**What Hvar's grid reads as at a glance:**

The grid should read as warm, considered, and food-positive. An outsider viewing 9 posts at once should see:
- Dominant warm tones: ivory, warm browns, the occasional Hvar red as accent
- Real food — identifiably Egyptian dishes — present in most frames
- People's hands or the product in use (not flat-lay isolation)
- Enough visual variety that it is interesting, enough visual consistency that it is clearly one brand

**Color rhythm guideline:**
- No more than 2 consecutive posts with strong Hvar red as the dominant color — this is a grid that builds warmth, not a grid that screams
- No more than 2 consecutive posts without food or a cooking action in frame
- Alternate between warm closeup (food/texture) and medium-distance (kitchen environment, chef in action) — do not post 4 extreme closeups in a row

**Content type rhythm (suggested, not mandatory):**

| Pattern | Frequency |
|---------|----------|
| Food result / recipe dish | Every 2–3 posts |
| Product in use / appliance in context | Every 3–4 posts |
| Chef content | Every 3–5 posts (depending on content availability) |
| Kitchen environment / lifestyle | Every 4–6 posts |
| Trust/value post | Every 5–8 posts — Instagram is not the primary trust-building channel |

## Post Formats

### Single Image

**When to use:** A strong single image that needs no sequence to tell its story. A dish result. A beautiful kitchen moment. A product in a compelling context.

**What scenes work:**
- The completed dish, still steaming, with the appliance visible in the background or in the frame periphery
- Hands in the act of cooking — the precise moment of the pour, the chop, the plate
- A product on a real kitchen surface (wood, marble, tile) with food or ingredients around it
- Close-up texture: the crust on bread, the caramel on كنافة, the color of hummus

**Composition:**
- RTL awareness: primary subject can be right-aligned (the eye enters from the right in Arabic reading)
- Negative space should feel intentional, not like something was cropped out
- The rule of thirds applies — center-composition reads static on Instagram

### Carousel

**When to use:** When there is a sequence worth following — a recipe step-by-step, a before-and-after, a product feature-by-feature, a "5 dishes you can make with this appliance."

**Multi-slide structure:**
- Slide 1: the hook — the result, the question, the most compelling visual in the sequence. This is what appears in the grid and what determines whether anyone swipes.
- Slides 2–4 (or more): the sequence — steps, features, dishes. Each slide should stand alone visually. If someone sees only this slide, it must still communicate something complete.
- Final slide: a summary or CTA. "شوفي المنتج" or "احفظي الوصفة" — one clear action.

**Swipe-encouraging copy:** The first caption line should hint that there is more to see: "اسحبي ← لتشوفي الطريقة خطوة بخطوة"

### Reels

**When to use:** When the content is action-based and benefits from motion — actual cooking, technique demonstration, product in use. Reels get priority placement in the algorithm and on the Explore page.

**Hook formula:** Same as TikTok (see `social/tiktok.md`) — the first 2 seconds must earn the watch. Show the food result, or show the most surprising or satisfying moment of the cooking process.

**Optimal duration:**
- Recipe Reel: 30–60 seconds
- Product technique tip: 15–30 seconds
- Chef tutorial: 45–90 seconds (chef's natural pacing, not artificially compressed)

**Caption style for Reels:** Shorter than feed post captions. The video carries the content. The caption is the context and the invitation: 2–4 lines, one question or CTA.

## Stories Strategy

Stories are the trust-building layer. They are where the audience gets the "behind the brand" access that makes the feed content feel more real.

**Story content types:**

**Product demo in 15 seconds:**
- Not a recipe — a single product capability. "شوفي بتعمل إيه في ١٥ ثانية."
- Show the action, show the result. Product label visible. No voiceover needed.
- Link sticker to product page when applicable.

**Recipe reveals:**
- Teaser story sequence: the dish in progress (raw/uncooked) → the mid-process → the result
- 3–5 slides building anticipation, ending with the finished dish and a swipe-up or link sticker to the full recipe or the associated product

**Poll templates:**
- "هتعمل إيه اليوم للأكل؟" with 2 real options — this is engagement that also gives brand intelligence about what the audience is cooking
- "عارفة إن الفريير الهوائي يعمل ده؟" followed by a reveal slide
- Design: brand colors (ivory background or Hvar red background), Cairo typeface, clean and uncluttered

**Appliance tips ("هل عرفتِ"):**
- A single useful tip about one of Hvar's product categories. "الخلاط يشتغل أحسن لو الماء قبل المكونات الصلبة."
- Not a product advertisement — a genuinely useful tip that builds authority
- Hvar logo small in a corner. Not in the center.

**Before/after:**
- The appliance does something to the ingredients. Show before: raw, unprocessed. Show after: the result.
- Works especially well for: blenders (whole vegetables → smooth hummus), air fryers (raw chicken → golden result), mixers (whole ingredients → cake batter)

### Story Design Standards

- Template consistency: use a defined set of 3–4 story templates for each content type, built in the brand visual system
- Cairo typeface, correct Arabic, correct RTL text alignment
- Never use Instagram's built-in default templates — they apply generic aesthetics that override the brand
- Story backgrounds: brand ivory, brand red, warm brown kitchen-toned photo backgrounds. Never solid black or white.
- Text over photos: always ensure contrast — the grain texture and warm photography can reduce text legibility. Add a translucent overlay behind text where needed.

## Caption Voice

Instagram captions for Hvar are shorter than Facebook captions. The image carries the content. The caption invites, contextualizes, or asks.

**Voice on Instagram:** Same warm authority as the brand, but at lower volume. More conversational, less explicit about the sell.

**Length guidance:**
- Recipe posts: 3–5 lines. The dish. One specific detail. One question or invitation. Optional hashtags separated.
- Product posts: 4–6 lines. The use case. One specific capability. Price or CTA if appropriate. Hashtags.
- Chef content: 2–3 lines. Credit the chef. The dish. Where to find more.
- Lifestyle/environment: 1–3 lines. Let the image breathe. A question to the audience.

**Caption structure:**
```
[First line: the hook — this is what shows before "more"]
[Second line onward: context, detail, invitation]
[CTA or question — one, not three]

[Hashtags — separated by blank line or in first comment]
```

**Caption tone:** Asking is better than directing. "جربتِ تعمليها في الفريير الهوائي؟" invites more engagement than "اشتري الفريير الهوائي دلوقتي."

**When a CTA is appropriate:** Product posts, Reels with a product link, Stories with a link sticker. Not every caption needs a buy CTA — brand awareness content can end with a question or nothing at all.

## Photography Art Direction

This is the most detailed section because Instagram is primarily a photography-judged platform. The photography quality determines whether the brand looks like what it claims to be.

### Lighting

**Target:** warm, natural, directional.

- **Best:** morning kitchen light from a window, or late afternoon golden hour. This is the light that makes food look like food that was cooked with care.
- **Acceptable:** warm artificial lighting (tungsten-balanced LED panels) that replicates the warm window-light effect
- **Never:** cold LED overhead lighting (typical kitchen fluorescent — this is the enemy of warm food photography), harsh studio flash, mixed color temperatures in the same frame

**Specific lighting for appliances:** position the product so the light source creates a shadow that gives it depth and three-dimensionality. A flat-lit product from the front looks cheap. A product lit from the side with a gentle shadow looks like it is made of real materials.

### Props

Egyptian kitchen objects, not international staging props:
- Real ceramic dishes and bowls (Egyptian pottery if available)
- Worn-but-good wooden cutting boards (not pristine mall-store cutting boards)
- Fresh ingredients — real herbs, onions with the papery skin, whole vegetables. Not glossy supermarket-display ingredients.
- Kitchen towels — linen, cotton, with natural creases. Not the perfectly folded dish towel of a stock photo.

**What NOT to use as props:**
- Minimalist Scandinavian cutting boards and marble surfaces that look like a IKEA product shoot
- Fresh flowers (unless specifically for Eid or seasonal content where this is culturally appropriate)
- Vintage/antique kitchen items that create an aspirational fantasy rather than a real kitchen
- International spices or foods that Egyptian home cooks don't recognize as part of their kitchen

### Angles

| Angle | When to use | What it communicates |
|-------|------------|---------------------|
| Overhead (flat lay) | Completed dishes, ingredient spread, process steps | Authority — the view of the cook who sees the whole picture |
| Eye-level | Lifestyle — the chef cooking, a woman at the kitchen counter | Presence — you are in the kitchen with her |
| Three-quarter (45 degrees) | Product shots, the finished dish from an interesting angle | Form — shows the product or food as a three-dimensional object |
| Extreme close-up | Texture — crust, caramel, grain of wood | Intimacy — the detail that makes the audience want to touch or taste |

### Color Grading Direction

Hvar photography should be processed to:
- **Warm, never cool:** if you're choosing between making a photo slightly warmer or slightly cooler, warm is always the right direction for this brand
- **Lifted shadows:** bring the shadow areas up slightly — dark, murky shadows read as cheap production. Lifted shadows read as editorial.
- **Controlled highlights:** do not blow out highlights — the warmth of the light source should be visible, not a white flare
- **Slight grain:** a very subtle film grain effect in post, which harmonizes with the CSS grain texture system in the brand design
- **Saturation:** not oversaturated food photography that looks artificial. A slightly elevated but natural saturation, emphasizing warm reds, oranges, and yellows (which happen to be the dominant colors of most Egyptian food)

**Reference tone:** warm documentary food photography, not advertising commercial food photography. The difference is the difference between a photo that makes you want to eat the food and a photo that makes you want to buy the product. The Hvar target is the first — because the second follows from it.

## Hashtag Strategy

### Brand Tags (always use)
- `#هفار` — the brand's primary Arabic tag
- `#مطبخ_هفار` — the kitchen content community tag
- `#وصفات_هفار` — recipe content

### Egyptian Cooking Community Tags
- `#وصفات_مصرية` — Egyptian recipes community
- `#مطبخ_مصري` — Egyptian kitchen
- `#شيف_سارة` — when featuring Chef Sara content
- `#وصفات` — general recipes (high volume, lower specificity)
- `#طبخ_بيتي` — home cooking
- `#اكل_مصري` — Egyptian food

### Product Category Tags
- `#فريير_هوائي` — air fryer content
- `#خلاط_كهربائي` — blender content
- (equivalent for other product categories)

### Usage Rules
- Maximum 15 hashtags per post — above this, posts begin to look spammy to the algorithm and to human viewers
- Preferred: 5–8 hashtags for recipe content, 8–12 for product posts
- Placement: in the caption after a blank line break (to keep the caption readable without the hashtag block), or in the first comment immediately after posting
- Never use hashtags unrelated to the content (broad irrelevant hashtag stuffing does not help reach and signals low-quality content to the algorithm)
- Avoid hashtags with very high volumes that are dominated by content unrelated to Egyptian home cooking — the reach in these is low relative to the noise

### Tags to Avoid
- `#recipe` (English — the audience is Arabic-primary, and English tags bring non-target reach)
- `#foodporn` — tone mismatch, and the audience skews conservative
- Generic high-volume tags with no Arabic cooking specificity that will bury the content instantly
